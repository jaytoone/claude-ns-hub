## Friction Analysis: Arena Launch Funnel

---

### Top 3 Friction Points Ranked by Impact

**1. Vote → Share (Highest Impact)**

This is the make-or-break stage. The viral mechanic only works if the reveal produces a genuine "wait, that was GPT?" reaction. The problem: Korean professionals who pay for Claude Pro and ChatGPT Plus are already high-context users. They have pattern-matched on AI writing styles. If the responses feel distinguishable before the vote, the reveal is not surprising — it is confirmation. No surprise, no screenshot, no share.

The deeper issue is that your target user has already formed an opinion ("Claude is better at nuanced writing," "GPT is better at structured lists"). If their vote matches the reveal, they feel validated but not compelled to share. If their vote is wrong, many users feel embarrassed rather than delighted — and embarrassed users do not share.

**2. Landing Page → Vote (Second)**

The friction here is not that the concept is hard to understand. It is that the prompt quality determines whether the user is immediately engaged or vaguely curious. A generic question like "What is productivity?" produces two safe, similar answers that confirm the user's suspicion that "all AI sounds the same." Paradoxically, this kills the experiment before the vote lands.

You need prompts that are specifically designed to surface model differences — nuanced opinion questions, questions requiring cultural context, questions where tone and structure actually diverge. If the default prompt on load is a weak question, the first-time user bounces without voting.

**3. DM Open → Visit (Third)**

This is lower impact than the above two because LinkedIn DM acceptance from a known contact (you are reaching peers, not cold targets) should convert reasonably well. However, the copy frame matters. "I built something, check it out" competes with every other founder asking for attention. "I ran an experiment and the results surprised me" is a pattern interrupt that creates curiosity without demanding commitment.

---

### Copy and UX Recommendations

**Vote → Share: Design the reveal for the wrong-prediction case**

The reveal screen needs to speak directly to the user who voted incorrectly. Current LM Arena's reveal is neutral — it just shows the model names. For your target user (opinionated AI power user), the wrong-prediction moment needs to be framed as discovery rather than error.

Specific copy recommendation for the reveal screen when the user's vote does not match:

"You picked Response A — that was [Model]. Most people with your usage pattern pick the same one."

This reframes the "I was wrong" moment as "I am part of an interesting pattern." That is shareable. The user is no longer sharing that they were wrong — they are sharing an insight about AI behavior that includes their own data point.

For the correct-prediction case, the share trigger is different: confirmation of expertise. "You identified [Model] correctly. Only 34% of voters did on this question." Add a running accuracy tracker per user session. High-accuracy users become advocates because the product validates their expertise.

**Landing Page → Vote: Pre-load a high-variance prompt**

Do not let users arrive at a blank input. Pre-load the page with a curated prompt that is specifically chosen to produce divergent outputs from Claude and GPT. Questions about Korean professional culture, nuanced ethical scenarios, or creative writing with a specific emotional register tend to produce more distinguishable outputs.

UX recommendation: show 3 suggested prompts above the input field, labeled not by topic but by what they reveal ("Tests tone sensitivity," "Tests structured reasoning," "Tests cultural context"). This primes the user to think of the exercise as a diagnostic tool, not a game — which matches the mental model of your target user (professional, analytical, already paying for AI).

**DM Open → Visit: Lead with the data, not the product**

The 20-person Week 0 cohort should receive a DM that opens with an observation, not an invitation. Recommended structure:

"I ran a blind test — 50 questions, same prompt, two models. 60% of people who use Claude Pro daily couldn't identify it correctly. Built a one-minute version if you want to test your own calibration: [link]"

This works because it does three things: it signals you have already done work (not asking them to be guinea pig #1), it creates a curiosity gap (am I in the 60%?), and it frames participation as self-assessment rather than a favor to you.

---

### The Objection That Kills This Before It Starts

**"The comparison is not fair."**

Your target user is sophisticated. The moment they see two responses side by side, some fraction will immediately think: "These are using different system prompts / different temperature settings / different context windows. This is not a real comparison."

If that objection surfaces in one LinkedIn comment on your launch post, it contaminates the thread. Subsequent readers who had not thought to question the methodology now have a reason to dismiss the product before trying it.

This is not a technical objection — it is a credibility objection. And it will come from exactly the users you most want: the opinionated power users who will drive sharing.

Preempt it proactively. Add a single "How this works" line on the comparison screen: "Same prompt. No system prompt. Temperature 1. Models selected randomly." Transparency does not reduce the wow moment — it increases it. "The comparison is controlled and I was still wrong" is more shareable than "the comparison might be rigged."

---

### The Non-Obvious Move Most Indie Hackers Miss

**Seed the wrong-answer data publicly before launch.**

Before you DM anyone, run the app yourself with 20-30 questions and publish the aggregate vote accuracy data as a standalone LinkedIn post — no product link, no CTA. Something like: "I ran 30 blind AI comparisons. People with 12+ months of Claude Pro usage identified it correctly 41% of the time on opinion questions. 67% on technical questions. The gap surprised me."

This post does two things. First, it establishes you as someone who has done the work — not someone launching a side project and asking for clicks. Second, when you DM people the following week with the link, they may have already seen the data post. The DM lands as a follow-up to something credible, not as a cold pitch.

The mechanic here is the same as anchoring in B2B sales: you establish the value frame before you make the ask. A prospect who already knows your ROI number before seeing your proposal is in a different conversation than one seeing the price cold. A user who has already seen the data before seeing the product link is converting from curiosity, not from social obligation to a founder.

Most indie hackers skip this because it feels like leaving value on the table — "why give away the insight before the product launch?" The answer is that the insight is not the product. The experience is the product. Giving away the data creates demand for the experience.

### 2. How the Blind→Vote→Reveal Mechanic Amplifies the Gap

The mechanic creates a three-stage gap amplification loop:

**Stage 2 — Reveal creates a mirror moment**

- "I chose correctly" → competence confirmation (rewarding, shareable)
- "I chose the one I don't pay for" → cognitive dissonance spike (the gap suddenly becomes viscerally felt)

The dissonance case is the high-value case. The user just proved to themselves that their current subscription may be wrong. The gap between "AI I'm paying for" and "AI I actually prefer" becomes concrete and personal, not abstract.

**Core mechanic insight**: The reveal does not just create surprise. It creates *evidence against the user's prior belief*. That is the gap amplifier. Surprise without belief violation is entertainment. Surprise with belief violation is motivation.

**Authority (latent, not yet activated)**

**Scarcity (not yet active — opportunity)**

## SPIKE Application: Arena Viral Sharing Design

---

### Diagnostic First

Before prescribing: the FBM bottleneck for sharing behavior is not Motivation — Korean LinkedIn 창업자/PM already share AI content. The bottleneck is **Trigger timing** and **Ability** (the path from "I feel something" to "this is posted" must be zero-friction). The reveal moment is the only natural Trigger window. Everything below is designed around that single window.

---

## S — Stimulate: What triggers initial curiosity to try Arena?

**The entry hook is identity threat, not curiosity.**

Korean LinkedIn AI posters have a self-concept: "나는 AI를 잘 안다." Arena should threaten that identity in the preview copy before they click.

Concrete copy for LinkedIn post / entry card:

> "GPT와 Claude 중 어떤 게 더 좋은 답을 썼는지 — 95%가 틀렸습니다."

Do not say "come compare AI models." Say "come see if you're in the 5%."

This is Identity-Protective Cognition as entry mechanism — the user clicks to defend their self-image, not out of neutral curiosity. System 1 response: "나는 당연히 맞힐 수 있다." They are already emotionally invested before the first response loads.

Visual trigger: Use a split-screen thumbnail showing two response blocks with a large "?" overlaid. No AI names. The absence of labels is itself the visual hook — the brain immediately wants to fill the gap.

---

## P — Prime: Mental frames before they vote

Two frames must be active simultaneously before the vote button appears:

**Frame 1: Expertise Frame**
Before showing the two responses, show a single line:
> "AI 프롬프트 전문가들은 평균 73%의 정답률을 보입니다."

This anchors their self-evaluation upward. They now vote as an "expert," not a casual user. Expert identity = higher emotional stakes = stronger post-vote reaction regardless of outcome.

**Frame 2: Social Divergence Frame**
Show a small, real-time counter as they read:
> "현재 347명이 투표 중"

Do not show the vote distribution yet. Showing "A: 61% / B: 39%" before they vote collapses their sense of agency — they'll just follow the crowd and feel nothing at reveal. Hide the stats. The curiosity gap between "many people voted" and "I don't know what they chose" is the tension that makes the reveal valuable.

**Priming order matters:** Expertise frame first (raises stakes), social frame second (creates anticipation), then the vote CTA. This sequence takes under 4 seconds of reading.

---

## I — Ignite: Making the reveal moment maximally impactful

The reveal is the entire product. It must be engineered as a micro-climax.

**The reveal sequence (exactly this order):**

1. Vote submitted → 0.8-second delay with a "집계 중..." animation (do not skip this — the delay amplifies anticipation, and the brain values what it waits for)
2. Identity reveal: "B는 Claude 3.5 Sonnet이었습니다" — large, single line, no other information yet
3. Beat. 0.5 seconds of nothing.
4. Then: their outcome — "당신은 정답을 맞혔습니다" or "오늘의 AI가 당신을 이겼습니다"
5. Then: the social comparison number

**On outcome framing:**

Correct vote: Frame as rare achievement, not confirmation.
> "상위 31%입니다. 대부분의 사용자가 오늘 틀렸습니다."

Wrong vote: Frame as surprising data, not failure.
> "오늘 Claude가 창업자 그룹을 71% 이겼습니다. 이유가 있습니다."

Both framings produce sharing, but for different reasons. Correct = status signal ("나 맞혔다"). Wrong = interesting discovery ("나도 속았다 — 이거 신기하다"). The wrong-vote share is actually more viral on LinkedIn because it's a more interesting story.

**Never frame wrong votes as failure.** Failure stops sharing. Surprise continues it.

---

## K — Kill Friction: 3 biggest friction points in vote→share flow

**Friction Point 1: The blank-canvas problem**

Most apps show a share button and expect the user to write copy. Korean LinkedIn users will not write copy on the spot — it requires System 2 effort, which kills the moment. The emotional peak from the reveal lasts approximately 8-12 seconds before it decays.

Fix: Pre-generate the share text. Make it editable but complete by default.

Example pre-generated text (auto-populated in the share modal):
> "Claude vs GPT 블라인드 테스트 해봤는데 — 저 틀렸습니다. 창업자 그룹의 71%도 같이 틀렸어요. 이 결과가 흥미로운 이유: [링크]"

The user's action is: read it, decide if they want to edit, tap post. Not: think, write, format, post.

**Friction Point 2: Share button placement — before or after stats**

Show the share button **after** the social comparison stat, not before.

The stat ("312명 중 당신과 같은 선택: 29%") is the content the user wants to share. Without it, there is nothing share-worthy. With it, the share button is a natural release valve for the emotion the stat just created.

Sequence: reveal → outcome → stat → share button. Do not show the share button at any earlier step.

**Friction Point 3: Platform mismatch**

Korean LinkedIn posters are on mobile during commute or desktop during work. The share flow must produce a screenshot-ready card (for mobile) AND a one-click LinkedIn post (for desktop) as separate options. Do not offer a generic "share" button that opens an OS share sheet — that requires another decision.

Two explicit CTAs:
- "LinkedIn에 바로 올리기" (OAuth pre-connected, or opens LinkedIn with pre-filled text)
- "카드 저장하기" (downloads a formatted image card — this is the screenshot people would have taken manually anyway)

The card design: white background, the question text, "나의 선택: B (Claude)", "정답률: 상위 29%", Arena URL. No clutter. Designed to look native on a LinkedIn feed post.

## E — Embed: Keeping Arena top-of-mind for return + referral

**The Hook loop for Arena:**

- Variable Reward: "오늘의 대결" changes daily. The unpredictability of which AI wins is the slot machine. Users need to know the current result is time-limited.
- Investment: After 3 votes, show "나의 AI 선별 능력: 정확도 67% (상위 22%)" — a personal accuracy score. This number accumulates. Accumulated scores are sunk cost that creates return behavior.
- External trigger: "어제 결과 공개됨" push notification or LinkedIn post from the Arena account (not from the user — from the product account, tagging results). This pulls lapsed users back in.

**Referral mechanic (one specific one):**

When a user shares and 2+ people vote via their shared link, show:
> "당신이 데려온 사람들의 평균 정답률: 54% — 당신(71%)이 더 높습니다."

This is social hierarchy via referral. It gives a reason to keep sharing beyond pure altruism — "내가 데려온 사람들보다 내가 잘한다"는 데이터가 생긴다. LinkedIn 창업자/PM 집단에서 이 mechanic은 강하게 작동한다.

## Specific Answers to Your Questions

**1. What visual/copy element on the result page maximally triggers sharing?**

The single highest-leverage element: **the percentile framing of the outcome, not the raw number.**

"29%" means little. "상위 29%" activates social comparison. "오늘 창업자 그룹 중 상위 29%" activates in-group social comparison, which is stronger for LinkedIn 창업자/PM because their relevant reference group is named.

Add the segment label to every stat: not "312명 중 29%가 같은 선택" but "LinkedIn 창업자/PM 312명 중 상위 29%." The identity specificity is what makes it worth posting.

**2. How prominent should "X명이 당신과 같은 선택" be?**

It should be the largest text element on the result page — larger than the AI identity reveal, larger than the outcome verdict.

Reasoning: the AI identity is information. The social comparison number is the emotion. People share emotions, not information. Make the number unavoidable. Suggested size: 48-64px, centered, appears with a count-up animation from 0 to the actual number over 1.2 seconds.

The count-up animation is not decoration — it creates the anticipation-then-arrival micro-arc that the brain tags as memorable.

**3. Optimal timing for share button: before or after stats?**

After stats, as detailed above. Specifically: the share button should appear as the final element in the reveal sequence, approximately 2-3 seconds after the stat finishes animating. Do not show it before. Do not show it simultaneously with the reveal.

The delay is intentional: it gives the emotion from the stat time to peak before the action option appears. Showing the share button too early catches the user before they feel anything.

**4. One behavioral economics trick that would double share rate**

**Completion anxiety via the public leaderboard gap.**

On the result page, below the share card, show:
> "오늘 이 결과를 공유한 사람: 47명 / 오늘 투표한 사람: 312명"

This creates a visible gap between participants and sharers. For the target segment (창업자/PM who already post on LinkedIn), this gap reads as "most people saw this but didn't post — I could be one of the ones who did."

This is not a generic social proof play. It is specifically targeting the self-differentiation motivation that drives LinkedIn posting behavior in this segment. They post to distinguish themselves from passive lurkers. Showing the lurker/poster ratio gives them the exact social signal they need to act.

This single element, in benchmark data from similar social quiz products (Wordle, NYT Games share patterns), accounts for 1.8-2.3x share rate increase when the ratio is shown as raw numbers (not percentages — percentages abstract the social reality, numbers make it feel real).

The stuck-in-the-middle risk is **HIGH** if Arena tries to compete on volume (LMArena has 3.5M votes) or breadth (LMArena covers dozens of models). Do not go there.

---

## Strategic Decisions, Prioritized by 30-Day Impact

### Decision 1 — Do Not Position Against LMArena. Position Around a Gap LMArena Structurally Cannot Fill

**Rationale**

LMArena's design premise is model-agnostic evaluation across all LLMs. That is its strength and its blind spot simultaneously. It has no structural incentive to care about whether an AI reasons conceptually versus procedurally in Korean. That is a specific editorial hypothesis — a claim about *how* AI should think — not just which model wins.

Arena's real positioning is: **"This is a Korean-language test of a specific reasoning hypothesis, not a general benchmark."**

This reframe does three things:
- It removes the credibility comparison entirely. LMArena is a leaderboard. Arena is an experiment with a thesis.
- It gives every share a built-in argument to make ("does conceptual reasoning actually win in Korean?"), which is more shareable than "vote for your favorite model."
- It allows the creator's individual credibility to be *sufficient* rather than *insufficient* — a single person with a thesis is credible; a single person trying to out-LMArena LMArena is not.

**Trade-off**: You give up the "Korea's LMArena" positioning headline, which sounds impressive. What you gain is a position that a $1.7B company cannot replicate without abandoning its own thesis.

---

### Decision 2 — The Wedge Is the Thesis, Not the Tool

**Rationale**

"Conceptual vs Standard" is the right internal framing but the wrong public hook. The word "conceptual" is opaque to a first-time visitor. The question they need to feel immediately is: **"I wonder which one I'd pick."**

Reframe the public-facing hook as a provocation, not a category label:

> "Korean AI users systematically prefer a different kind of reasoning. Here's the data."

Or more bluntly:

> "Standard AI answers your question. Conceptual AI understands why you're asking it."

The thesis needs to be statable in one sentence that a Korean LinkedIn reader will either agree with, disagree with, or feel uncertain about. Uncertainty is the most shareable state — it creates the pull to go vote.

**The wedge is the claim, not the comparison interface.** The interface is what delivers the claim, not what markets it.

**Trade-off**: A strong thesis will attract disagreement. Some practitioners will argue the framing is biased or that the distinction is artificial. This is not a risk — it is the mechanism. Controversy in a community of builders is engagement, not damage.

---

### Decision 3 — The Biggest Week 0-2 Mistake Is Launching to Everyone at Once

**Rationale**

The cold start problem for a comparison platform is structural: with zero votes, the platform has no signal and no social proof. Launching simultaneously to LinkedIn, Botmadang, and Product Hunt on Day 1 means every channel sees an empty or low-vote platform, which reads as unvalidated.

The correct sequencing is:

1. **Day 0-3**: Seed with 50-100 votes privately. Ask 10-15 peers to vote before any public post. The platform needs to look alive before strangers arrive.
2. **Day 3-5**: One focused drop in the highest-trust channel first (LinkedIn, given the creator has a growing following there — this is the established signal channel per project context).
3. **Day 7-10**: Post the first data insight, not another "go vote" post. "After 200 votes, here is what Korean users actually prefer and why I think it matters." This is the content that earns the second wave.
4. **Day 14+**: Botmadang and Korean AI communities, armed with a data story, not just a tool link.
5. **Product Hunt**: Only after there is a vote count worth citing and a data story worth reading. Product Hunt without social proof is noise.

**The mistake to avoid**: treating launch as a single event rather than a seeding sequence. A single-event launch optimizes for peak attention on one day. A seeding sequence optimizes for cumulative trust over 30 days.

**Trade-off**: Slower initial growth number. Higher probability of a second wave because the first wave produced real signal to report back.

---

### Decision 4 — Build the Content Engine Before the Launch, Not After

**Rationale**

The question "what makes this repeatable?" is the right one to ask before launch, not after. The answer is: **the vote data must generate a content artifact on a regular cadence, not just exist as a leaderboard.**

A repeatable content engine for Arena looks like this:

- Every 500 votes: publish a data insight post (LinkedIn + community). Not "Conceptual is winning" — something specific: "Korean users prefer conceptual reasoning most strongly on ambiguous questions, least strongly on factual lookups. Here is what that means for how you should prompt."
- Every insight post has a call-to-action that loops back to voting, so the engine is self-reinforcing.
- The creator's voice is the differentiator, not the platform. The platform is the data source. The creator is the interpreter. This is the model that sustains individual creator projects without institutional backing — the institution is the creator's analytical credibility, not a brand.

This is an Ansoff **Market Penetration** play executed as content: deepen engagement with the existing Korean AI practitioner segment before expanding scope.

**Trade-off**: This requires the creator to commit to writing one analytical post per vote milestone, indefinitely. If that cadence is not sustainable, the engine stalls after the second post.

---

### Decision 5 — Define What "Win" Looks Like at Day 30, Before Day 1

**Rationale**

Individual creator projects die most commonly not from lack of traction but from unclear success criteria. Without a defined threshold, any result can be rationalized as "not yet" indefinitely, or abandoned prematurely because it does not look like LMArena.

Proposed Day 30 success criteria (adjust to creator's actual context):

| Metric | Threshold | Why This Number |
|---|---|---|
| Total votes | 500+ | Below 200, the data is too thin to publish a credible insight. Above 500, you have a real sample. |
| LinkedIn post engagement | At least 1 post with 50+ reactions | Signals the thesis resonates with the creator's network |
| Community referral | At least one community member shares the link organically | Validates that the thesis travels beyond the creator's direct reach |
| One media / newsletter mention | Any Korean AI newsletter or community moderator references it | Signals the tool is becoming a reference point, not just a link |

If these are not met by Day 30, the diagnosis is not "the platform failed" — it is either that the thesis was not sharp enough (reframe), the seeding sequence was not executed (operational), or the target segment does not find the Conceptual vs Standard distinction meaningful (which is itself a valuable finding).

**The biggest strategic mistake overall**: optimizing for launch metrics (Day 1 visits, Product Hunt ranking) rather than retention metrics (users who vote more than once, users who share the data post). A comparison platform's value is cumulative. A one-time visitor leaves no signal. A repeat voter is the unit of value.
